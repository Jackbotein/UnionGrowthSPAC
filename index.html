The problem

High-opportunity cities are locked behind land scarcity.

Housing underproduction reflects a gap between the socially optimal quantity of housing and the supply of housing that maximizes the private utility of individuals with disproportionate political influence - which is namely municipal residents who frequently attend zoning approval meetings. This creates a trajedy of the commons whereby housing, a public good is supplied below its socially optimal level creating deadweight loss. The core bottleneck is not only materials, labor, or financing: it is the legal scarcity of parcels entitled for multifamily residential use in high-opportunity communities alogn with the ability to develop in a profitable manner due to affordable unit restrictions.

Investment thesis

Housing abundance becomes an investable public-market thesis.

Boston Union Growth SPAC proposes that organized labor and financial investors can form a new urban growth coalition: labor helps solve the political bottleneck to multifamily housing, while public-market capital finances the acquisition of underutilized municipal land. This land will then be removed from zoning constraints that inhibit high rise housing and multifamily rental housing at market-rents through leveraging union political power.

Risk statement

This investment opportunity is not all upside. Entitlement risk is real.

Projected land appreciation depends on acquisition execution, public approvals, zoning outcomes, developer demand, interest rates, construction feasibility, legal constraints, community opposition, and broader market conditions.
